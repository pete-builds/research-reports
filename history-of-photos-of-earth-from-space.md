## Current Status

- **Artemis II** returned the first human-taken full-disk Earth photo since 1972, titled "Hello, World," captured by Reid Wiseman on April 3, 2026, using a Nikon D5. The crew also captured a historic "Earthset" image on April 6, 2026, the first showing both Moon and Earth from thousands of miles beyond lunar orbit. [source: [Wikipedia - Hello, World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hello,_World_(photograph)); [Space.com](https://www.space.com/space-exploration/human-spaceflight/artemis-2-captures-historic-earthset-photo-space-photo-of-the-day-for-april-7-2026)]
- **DSCOVR's EPIC camera** continues to publish ~12 full-disk Earth images per day from the L1 Lagrange point, approximately one million miles from Earth. Images are publicly available 12 to 36 hours after capture. [source: [NASA EPIC](https://epic.gsfc.nasa.gov/about/epic)]
- **Landsat 9**, launched September 27, 2021, continues the 53-year Landsat program, the longest-running continuous Earth observation satellite series. [source: [USGS Landsat](https://www.usgs.gov/landsat-missions/landsat-satellite-missions)]
- NASA's 2020 reprocessing of the 1990 **Pale Blue Dot** image using modern software produced a clearer version while preserving the original data, renewing public interest. [source: [Wikipedia - Pale Blue Dot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pale_Blue_Dot)]
- The "overview effect," the cognitive shift astronauts experience when seeing Earth from space, remains an active area of psychological research, including virtual reality simulations aimed at replicating the experience. [source: [Wikipedia - Overview effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overview_effect)]

## Findings

### 1. The Earliest Images (1946-1954)

The first photograph of Earth from space was captured on **October 24, 1946**, when a captured German V-2 rocket (designated V-2 No. 13) launched from White Sands Missile Range, New Mexico, reached a maximum altitude of 65 miles (105 km), above the Karman line. A **DeVry 35mm black-and-white motion picture camera**, engineered by Clyde Holliday of the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory, was set to take one frame every 1.5 seconds. The rocket slammed back into the desert at 500 feet per second, destroying the camera, but the film survived inside a steel cassette. [source: [Wikipedia - V-2 No. 13](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V-2_No._13); [Smithsonian Air & Space](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/air-space-magazine/the-first-photo-from-space-13721411/)]

General Electric assembled and launched the rocket using captured German components and re-manufactured parts as part of the Hermes program. Between 1946 and 1950, researchers collected over 1,000 images of Earth from V-2 flights. The pictures were not released until 1950, when National Geographic published them. [source: [Smithsonian](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/smart-news/70-years-ago-today-nazi-rockets-and-american-scientists-took-first-photo-earth-space-180960890/)]

On **March 7, 1947**, a subsequent V-2 produced the first dedicated and published Earth observation images. On **October 5, 1954**, an Aerobee AJ10-24 sounding rocket captured the first color photomosaic from space, assembled from 117 images taken at approximately 100 miles altitude. [source: [Wikipedia - Timeline of first images of Earth from space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_first_images_of_Earth_from_space)]

### 2. The Space Race Era (1959-1967)

**Explorer 6** (August 14, 1959) returned the first photograph of Earth from orbit, a crude, smudged image of the central Pacific Ocean transmitted over 40 minutes from an altitude of approximately 17,000 miles. The satellite's "paddlewheel" design with solar cells was a proof-of-concept for orbital Earth imaging. [source: [Drew Ex Machina](https://www.drewexmachina.com/2024/08/14/first-pictures-view-of-the-earth-from-nasas-explorer-6-august-14-1959/)]

**TIROS-1** (April 1, 1960) became the first dedicated weather satellite, returning the first television image of Earth from space and eventually producing 23,000 images from its 450-mile orbit, most of sufficient quality for weather forecasting. [source: [Wikipedia - Timeline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_first_images_of_Earth_from_space)]

**Cosmonaut Gherman Titov** aboard Vostok 2 (August 6, 1961) became the first human to photograph Earth from space. [source: [Wikipedia - Timeline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_first_images_of_Earth_from_space)]

**ATS-3** (November 10, 1967) produced the first full-disk "true color" image of Earth from geostationary orbit. This image appeared on the cover of the first *Whole Earth Catalog* in 1968. [source: [Wikipedia - Timeline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_first_images_of_Earth_from_space)]

### 3. Iconic Photographs

#### Earthrise (December 24, 1968)

Taken by **William Anders** aboard Apollo 8 at **16:39:39.3 UTC** during the mission's fourth lunar orbit using a **modified Hasselblad 500 EL** with electric motor drive, a 250mm lens, and custom 70mm Kodak Ektachrome film. The moment was unplanned: Anders spotted Earth emerging above the lunar horizon, shot a black-and-white frame, then urgently asked Jim Lovell for color film. Camera settings were 1/250th second at f/11. [source: [Wikipedia - Earthrise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earthrise)]

For years, mission commander Frank Borman claimed credit, writing in his 1988 autobiography: "One of the most famous pictures in photographic history, taken after I grabbed the camera away from Bill Anders." Borman later recanted when NASA transcript analysis confirmed Anders took all three Earth-rise images. [source: [Wikipedia - Earthrise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earthrise)]

Wilderness photographer Galen Rowell called it "the most influential environmental photograph ever taken." It was made into a U.S. postage stamp in 1969 and appeared on posters during the first Earth Day in April 1970. [source: [Wikipedia - Earthrise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earthrise)]

#### The Blue Marble (December 7, 1972)

Captured approximately 5 hours after the Apollo 17 launch at about **05:39 AM EST**, from roughly **29,400 km (18,300 miles)** from Earth. Equipment: **70mm Hasselblad 500 EL** with an **80mm Zeiss planar lens**, Kodak SO-368 Ektachrome film, f/2.8 aperture, 1/250 second shutter speed. [source: [Wikipedia - The Blue Marble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Blue_Marble)]

NASA officially credits the entire Apollo 17 crew, but analysis by Eric Hartwell and a 2013 interview confirm **Harrison Schmitt** as the photographer, motivated by his amateur meteorology hobby. The image shows the Mediterranean Sea to Antarctica, including nearly all of Africa's coastline, Madagascar, the Arabian Peninsula, and a cyclone in the Indian Ocean. [source: [Wikipedia - The Blue Marble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Blue_Marble)]

Released on December 23, 1972, it became "one of the most reproduced images in history" and a defining symbol of the 1970s environmental movement. NASA later produced Blue Marble composites from satellite data in 2002 (1 km/pixel), 2005 (500 m/pixel monthly composites), and 2012 (from Suomi NPP satellite). [source: [Wikipedia - The Blue Marble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Blue_Marble); [NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/image-article/apollo-17-astronauts-capture-iconic-blue-marble-50-years-ago/)]

#### Pale Blue Dot (February 14, 1990)

Voyager 1 turned its **narrow-angle camera** (a 1500mm f/8.5 catadioptric cassegrain telescope) back toward Earth from **6.055 billion km (3.762 billion miles, 40.47 AU)** away. Three exposures through blue, green, and violet filters (0.72, 0.48, and 0.72 seconds) were composited. Earth occupies less than one pixel (0.12 of a pixel) in the 640,000-pixel frame. [source: [Wikipedia - Pale Blue Dot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pale_Blue_Dot)]

The photograph exists because of **Carl Sagan**, who proposed it during Voyager's 1980 Saturn flyby. NASA resisted, citing lack of scientific value and institutional priorities, until NASA Administrator Richard Truly intervened to ensure the image was captured. Sagan's 1994 book *Pale Blue Dot* featured his famous reflection: "Look again at that dot. That's here. That's home. That's us." [source: [Wikipedia - Pale Blue Dot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pale_Blue_Dot); [Cornell University](https://news.cornell.edu/stories/2020/02/iconic-pale-blue-dot-photo-carl-sagans-idea-turns-30)]

### 4. The Satellite and Digital Era

**Landsat 1** (originally ERTS-1) launched **July 23, 1972**, beginning the world's longest-running Earth observation satellite program. Its two instruments, the Return Beam Vidicon (RBV) and Multispectral Scanner System (MSS), delivered 80-meter resolution digital imagery that proved superior to analog camera systems and revolutionized Earth science. The program now spans nine satellites, with Landsat 5 setting a Guinness World Record for longest-operating Earth observation satellite (28 years, 10 months). [source: [NASA Landsat](https://science.nasa.gov/mission/landsat-1/); [USGS](https://www.usgs.gov/landsat-missions/landsat-satellite-missions)]

**CORONA/Discoverer 14** (August 18, 1960) was the first space-based Earth observation system using photographic film physically recovered from orbit. The classified **KH-7 Gambit** program (1963) achieved the first sub-meter resolution satellite photography. The **KH-11** system (1977) introduced real-time satellite imagery. [source: [Wikipedia - Timeline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_first_images_of_Earth_from_space)]

**IKONOS** (October 13, 1999) delivered the first commercial sub-meter resolution imagery, appearing in the New York Times and opening the door to commercial Earth observation. [source: [Wikipedia - Timeline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_first_images_of_Earth_from_space)]

### 5. Modern Earth Observation

**DSCOVR** launched February 11, 2015, aboard a SpaceX Falcon 9 and reached the L1 Lagrange point (about one million miles from Earth) on June 8, 2015. Its EPIC instrument is a **4-megapixel CCD camera and telescope** that takes images through 10 narrowband filters (ultraviolet to near-infrared). Three single-color images are combined into photographic-quality composites equivalent to 12 megapixels, with 10-15 km/pixel resolution. About 12 images are published daily, enabling global study of vegetation, ozone, aerosols, and cloud dynamics. [source: [NASA EPIC](https://epic.gsfc.nasa.gov/about/epic); [Wikipedia - DSCOVR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory)]

DSCOVR originated as **Triana**, a 1998 mission conceived by Vice President Al Gore to provide near-continuous public views of Earth. [source: [Wikipedia - DSCOVR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory)]

**Artemis II** (April 2026) produced two landmark images. "Hello, World" (April 3) was captured by Reid Wiseman with a **Nikon D5 at 14-24mm f/2.8**, f/4, ISO 51,200, 1/4-second exposure, showing city lights, auroras at both poles, and airglow along Earth's limb. It was the first full-disk human-taken photograph of Earth since Apollo 17 in 1972. "Earthset" (April 6) showed both Earth and the Moon from thousands of miles beyond lunar orbit, another first. [source: [Wikipedia - Hello, World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hello,_World_(photograph)); [Space.com](https://www.space.com/space-exploration/human-spaceflight/artemis-2-captures-historic-earthset-photo-space-photo-of-the-day-for-april-7-2026)]

### 6. Cultural and Scientific Impact

**The Overview Effect.** Coined by Frank White in his 1987 book *The Overview Effect: Space Exploration and Human Evolution*, the term describes the cognitive shift astronauts experience when seeing Earth from space. A 2018 survey of 39 astronauts found that humanistic changes predominated over spiritual ones, and that viewing Earth correlated with subsequent environmental activism. [source: [Wikipedia - Overview effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overview_effect)]

Key astronaut accounts capture the experience:
- **Yuri Gagarin (1961):** "Circling the Earth in a spaceship, I marveled at the beauty of our planet. People of the world, let us safeguard and enhance this beauty, and not destroy it!"
- **Michael Collins (Apollo 11, 1969):** Described Earth as "tiny, it's shiny, it's beautiful, it's home, and it's fragile."
- **Edgar Mitchell (Apollo 14, 1971):** Called it an "explosion of awareness" and an "ecstasy" involving "oneness and connectedness."
[source: [Wikipedia - Overview effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overview_effect)]

**Environmental Movement.** Within two years of the 1968 Earthrise image, Greenpeace and Friends of the Earth were founded, and 20 million people participated in the first Earth Day on April 22, 1970. The Blue Marble reinforced this momentum during peak environmental activism in the early 1970s. These photographs are widely credited with shifting the conservation movement from local pollution concerns to a global consciousness about Earth's fragility. [source: [Long Now Foundation](https://longnow.org/ideas/whole-earth-overview-effect/); [Wikipedia - Overview effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overview_effect)]

**Scientific Applications.** Earth observation imagery now underpins climate science, agriculture, urban planning, disaster response, deforestation monitoring, and military intelligence. The Landsat archive alone provides 53+ years of continuous global land surface data. DSCOVR enables daily global studies of atmospheric composition. Commercial providers like those following IKONOS deliver sub-meter imagery for civilian and commercial use. [source: [NASA Landsat](https://science.nasa.gov/mission/landsat/); [NASA EPIC](https://epic.gsfc.nasa.gov/about/epic)]
