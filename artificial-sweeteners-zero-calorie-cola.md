## Current Status

- The FDA continues to affirm the safety of all six approved artificial sweeteners (aspartame, sucralose, acesulfame potassium, saccharin, neotame, advantame) at levels within their established ADIs. [source: [FDA - Aspartame and Other Sweeteners in Food](https://www.fda.gov/food/food-additives-petitions/aspartame-and-other-sweeteners-food)]
- In July 2023, IARC classified aspartame as "possibly carcinogenic" (Group 2B), but JECFA simultaneously reaffirmed the ADI of 40 mg/kg/day, stating safety is "not a major concern at the doses which are commonly used." [source: [WHO - Aspartame hazard and risk assessment results released](https://www.who.int/news/item/14-07-2023-aspartame-hazard-and-risk-assessment-results-released)]
- In May 2023, WHO recommended against using non-sugar sweeteners for weight control, citing lack of long-term weight loss benefit and potential risks of type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease. [source: [WHO - Non-sugar sweeteners guideline](https://www.who.int/news/item/15-05-2023-who-advises-not-to-use-non-sugar-sweeteners-for-weight-control-in-newly-released-guideline)]
- 2024-2025 research continues to find associations between artificial sweeteners and gut microbiome disruption, though individual responses vary significantly. [source: [PMC - Impacts of non-nutritive sweeteners on the human microbiome (2025)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12020452/)]
- A large 2024 PMC clinical review concluded that "the majority of research data show no link between the use of artificial sweeteners and cancer risk" (meta-analysis OR = 0.91, 95% CI = 0.75-1.11). [source: [PMC - The Impact of Artificial Sweeteners on Human Health and Cancer Association](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10822749/)]
- Erythritol (a sugar alcohol found in some "zero sugar" products, though not typically in major cola brands) has drawn concern from Cleveland Clinic studies linking it to increased platelet reactivity and cardiovascular event risk. [source: [Cleveland Clinic - Erythritol cardiovascular risk (2024)](https://consultqd.clevelandclinic.org/evidence-mounts-that-sugar-substitute-erythritol-raises-cardiovascular-risk)]

## Findings

### 1. What Are the Sweeteners in Zero-Calorie Colas?

The major zero-calorie cola products use different sweetener blends:

| Product | Sweeteners Used |
|---------|----------------|
| **Coca-Cola Zero Sugar** | Aspartame + acesulfame potassium (ace-K); stevia added in some markets as of late 2024 |
| **Diet Coke** | Aspartame only |
| **Pepsi Zero Sugar** | Aspartame + acesulfame potassium |
| **Diet Pepsi** | Sucralose (switched from aspartame in 2015, though formulations vary by market) |

[source: [Coca-Cola US - Zero Sugar Products](https://www.coca-cola.com/us/en/brands/coca-cola/products/zero); [Wikipedia - Pepsi Zero Sugar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pepsi_Zero_Sugar); [Wikipedia - Coca-Cola Zero Sugar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coca-Cola_Zero_Sugar)]

The dominant sweeteners you are consuming in a typical zero-calorie cola are **aspartame** and **acesulfame potassium (ace-K)**, with sucralose in some Pepsi products.

### 2. Health Effects by Sweetener

**Aspartame**

- The most studied artificial sweetener. Over 100 studies reviewed by the FDA before approval. [source: [FDA](https://www.fda.gov/food/food-additives-petitions/aspartame-and-other-sweeteners-food)]
- IARC Group 2B ("possibly carcinogenic") based on limited evidence for hepatocellular carcinoma (liver cancer). This is the same category as aloe vera and pickled vegetables. [source: [WHO/IARC July 2023](https://www.who.int/news/item/14-07-2023-aspartame-hazard-and-risk-assessment-results-released)]
- Metabolized into phenylalanine, aspartic acid, and methanol. The methanol amounts are small (less than what you get from a glass of tomato juice). [source: [American Cancer Society](https://www.cancer.org/cancer/risk-prevention/chemicals/aspartame.html)]
- Contraindicated for people with phenylketonuria (PKU), a rare genetic disorder. [source: [FDA](https://www.fda.gov/food/food-additives-petitions/aspartame-and-other-sweeteners-food)]

**Acesulfame Potassium (Ace-K)**

- Often used in combination with aspartame (they enhance each other's sweetness).
- A French cohort study (NutriNet-Sante, 102,865 participants) found an association between ace-K consumption and increased overall cancer risk, though this is observational data and does not prove causation. [source: [PMC - Artificial Sweeteners and Human Health Clinical Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10822749/)]
- Some studies have linked ace-K to coronary heart disease risk, though evidence remains preliminary. [source: [Harvard Health - Sugar substitutes cardiovascular concerns](https://www.health.harvard.edu/heart-health/sugar-substitutes-new-cardiovascular-concerns)]

**Sucralose**

- A 2022 study found that 10-week sucralose consumption induced gut dysbiosis and altered glucose and insulin levels in healthy young adults. [source: [PMC - Exploring the Long-Term Effect of Artificial Sweeteners on Metabolic Health](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11501561/)]
- Concerns about sucralose generating potentially harmful compounds when heated (above 119C/247F), though this is not relevant to drinking cold beverages. [source: [PMC - Sucralose, A Synthetic Organochlorine Sweetener](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3856475/)]
- Associated with reduced populations of beneficial gut bacteria (Bifidobacterium, Lactobacillus). [source: [PMC - Artificial Sweeteners: A Double-Edged Sword for Gut Microbiome](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9891/13/4/115)]

**Stevia (steviol glycosides)**

- Plant-derived, generally considered the most "natural" option among non-sugar sweeteners.
- FDA has accepted GRAS (Generally Recognized as Safe) status for high-purity steviol glycosides (95%+ purity). [source: [FDA](https://www.fda.gov/food/food-additives-petitions/aspartame-and-other-sweeteners-food)]
- Less long-term human data compared to aspartame or sucralose, but no significant safety red flags in current literature.

### 3. Acceptable Daily Intake (ADI) Guidelines

ADI values represent the amount that can be consumed daily over a lifetime without appreciable health risk. These are set with large safety margins (typically 100x below the level where adverse effects were observed in animal studies).

| Sweetener | FDA ADI (mg/kg/day) | JECFA/EFSA ADI (mg/kg/day) |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------------------|
| **Aspartame** | 50 | 40 |
| **Acesulfame-K** | 15 | 15 (EFSA: 9) |
| **Sucralose** | 5 | 15 (JECFA) |
| **Stevia** | 4 (as steviol equivalents) | 4 (as steviol equivalents) |
| **Saccharin** | 15 | 5 |

[source: [FDA - Aspartame and Other Sweeteners in Food](https://www.fda.gov/food/food-additives-petitions/aspartame-and-other-sweeteners-food); [European Commission - ADI of sweeteners](https://knowledge4policy.ec.europa.eu/health-promotion-knowledge-gateway/sugars-sweeteners-7_en)]

### 4. How Many Cans Per Day Is Safe?

For a **70 kg (154 lb) adult** drinking standard 12 oz (355 ml) cans:

**Based on aspartame ADI alone (the binding constraint in most diet colas):**

- FDA ADI (50 mg/kg): 70 kg x 50 = 3,500 mg/day. At ~180-200 mg aspartame per can, that is roughly **17-19 cans/day** before exceeding the FDA limit.
- JECFA/EFSA ADI (40 mg/kg): 70 kg x 40 = 2,800 mg/day. That is roughly **14-15 cans/day**.
- WHO/JECFA's own calculation: **9-14 cans/day** to exceed ADI (accounting for 200-300 mg per can). [source: [WHO - Aspartame assessment July 2023](https://www.who.int/news/item/14-07-2023-aspartame-hazard-and-risk-assessment-results-released)]

**Practical recommendation (accounting for emerging research beyond just ADI):**

- **1-2 cans per day**: Generally considered safe by most health authorities. No credible evidence of harm at this level.
- **3-4 cans per day**: Still well within ADI limits, but some observational studies begin to show associations with metabolic changes at higher consumption levels.
- **5+ cans per day**: While still technically below the ADI, this level of consumption edges into territory where emerging concerns about gut microbiome disruption, insulin signaling, and cardiovascular markers become more relevant.

**Confidence level: Medium.** The ADI math is straightforward and high-confidence. The practical thresholds are informed by observational studies that cannot prove causation.

### 5. Recent Research and Controversies

**The IARC "Possibly Carcinogenic" Classification (July 2023)**

This generated significant media attention but requires context. Group 2B means "limited evidence" of carcinogenicity in humans. The same category includes aloe vera, pickled vegetables, and working in the dry cleaning industry. Importantly, IARC assesses *hazard* (can it cause cancer under any circumstance?) not *risk* (how likely is it to cause cancer at real-world exposure levels?). JECFA, assessing risk, did not change the ADI. [source: [WHO July 2023](https://www.who.int/news/item/14-07-2023-aspartame-hazard-and-risk-assessment-results-released)]

**WHO Recommendation Against Sweeteners for Weight Control (May 2023)**

WHO's systematic review found no long-term weight loss benefit from substituting sugar with non-sugar sweeteners, and flagged potential associations with type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease from long-term use. This is a conditional recommendation (low certainty evidence). [source: [WHO May 2023](https://www.who.int/news/item/15-05-2023-who-advises-not-to-use-non-sugar-sweeteners-for-weight-control-in-newly-released-guideline)]

**Gut Microbiome Disruption**

A growing body of research (2022-2025) shows artificial sweeteners can alter gut bacterial composition. A 2025 review documented reductions in beneficial Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium populations and increases in species associated with dysbiosis. However, individual responses vary significantly: some people are "responders" who show impaired glycemic responses, while others show no effect. [source: [PMC - Impacts of non-nutritive sweeteners on the human microbiome (2025)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12020452/); [PMC - Artificial Sweeteners: A Double-Edged Sword for Gut Microbiome (2025)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12025785/)]

**Cardiovascular Concerns**

A French cohort study (NutriNet-Sante) found associations between artificial sweetener consumption and cerebrovascular events (aspartame) and coronary heart disease (ace-K, sucralose). Separately, Cleveland Clinic studies in 2023-2024 linked erythritol (a sugar alcohol, not typically in colas) to increased platelet reactivity and cardiovascular events. [source: [Harvard Health](https://www.health.harvard.edu/heart-health/sugar-substitutes-new-cardiovascular-concerns); [Cleveland Clinic (2024)](https://consultqd.clevelandclinic.org/evidence-mounts-that-sugar-substitute-erythritol-raises-cardiovascular-risk)]

### 6. Practical Recommendations

1. **At 1-2 cans per day**, you are consuming a small fraction of the ADI. No regulatory body or major study has identified meaningful risk at this level. This is a reasonable daily amount.

2. **Water is still the best zero-calorie drink.** If you are using diet cola to replace sugared soda, that is a net positive. If you are choosing between diet cola and water, water wins.

3. **The "diet soda paradox" is observational.** Studies linking diet soda to weight gain, diabetes, or cardiovascular disease are mostly observational. People who drink more diet soda may already have higher baseline metabolic risk (reverse causation).

4. **Rotate if you are concerned.** If gut microbiome effects worry you, varying your sweetener exposure (rather than consuming the same one daily) is a reasonable precautionary approach, though there is no strong evidence this helps.

5. **Watch for individual responses.** If you notice digestive changes, headaches, or blood sugar issues correlated with diet soda consumption, that is worth paying attention to regardless of what population-level studies say.

6. **People with PKU must avoid aspartame.** This is the one absolute medical contraindication.

## Confidence Assessment

### High Confidence
- ADI values and regulatory positions (FDA, JECFA, EFSA) are well-documented and current
- Aspartame is safe at typical consumption levels (1-3 cans/day) for people without PKU
- IARC Group 2B classification reflects limited evidence, not established risk
- The math on "how many cans to exceed ADI" (9-19 depending on body weight and standard used)
- No credible evidence links moderate diet soda consumption to cancer in humans (meta-analysis OR = 0.91)

### Medium Confidence
- Gut microbiome disruption effects are real but clinical significance at moderate intake is unclear
- Long-term cardiovascular associations are observational and may reflect confounding
- WHO's recommendation against sweeteners for weight control is based on low-certainty evidence
- The practical recommendation of 1-2 cans/day as a comfortable safe zone (well-supported but not a formal guideline)

## Open Questions

- Will IARC's Group 2B classification lead to regulatory changes? So far, no agency has changed ADI levels in response.
- What are the long-term (10+ year) effects of daily artificial sweetener consumption on the gut microbiome, and do those translate to clinical outcomes?
- Does the combination of aspartame + ace-K (as used in Coke Zero and Pepsi Zero) behave differently than either sweetener alone? Most studies examine individual sweeteners in isolation.
- Are there meaningful differences in health outcomes between aspartame-based colas and sucralose-based colas?
- Will the 2025 gut microbiome research lead to personalized recommendations (e.g., identifying "responders" who should avoid artificial sweeteners)?
