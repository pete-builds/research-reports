## Current Status

- **IFLA Statement on Libraries and Artificial Intelligence** (Sept 17, 2020) is the earliest international association statement; AI literacy is treated as a four-part competency: how AI/ML work, societal impacts, personal data management, and Media and Information Literacy ([source](https://repository.ifla.org/bitstreams/8d21df6c-e61b-41a5-b089-0353a5552dbd/download)).
- **ACRL AI Competencies for Academic Library Workers** was approved by the ACRL Board on October 3, 2025 and is now the canonical U.S. academic-library framework. It is structured as 5 mindsets and 18 competencies across 4 categories (Ethics, Knowledge & Understanding, Analysis & Evaluation, Use & Application), and explicitly anchors itself to the ACRL Framework for Information Literacy ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2025-10/acrl_ai_competencies.pdf)).
- **ALA Guidance on the Use of Artificial Intelligence in Libraries** is in public-comment draft as of April 24, 2026 (comments closed May 8, 2026). It is built on ALA's Core Values and a Floridi-derived ethics appendix, and explicitly contemplates an "AI Literacy Framework" but defers it ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2026-04/AI%20Working%20Group%20-%20%20DRAFT%20for%20ALA%20Board%20Council%20and%20Members.pdf)).
- **The ACRL Framework for Information Literacy for Higher Education (2016) is being revised** by a joint task force; AI is one of the explicit prompts for revision but the task force position is that the Framework's threshold concepts are "robust and remain relevant" and that AI should be added as references and examples within existing frame language rather than as new frames ([source](https://crln.acrl.org/index.php/crlnews/article/view/27281/35079)).
- **The dominant scholarly framing across JAL, JASIST Proceedings, and C&RL News (2024-2025) treats academic librarians as natural AI-literacy educators by extension of information-literacy expertise**, not as new actors. LaFlamme's four-tier scaffold (JAL 2025), Ko et al.'s LibGuide analysis (JASIST 2024), and Lo's framework (CR&L News 2025) all explicitly invoke the ACRL IL Framework as a foundation for AI literacy ([source](https://colab.ws/articles/10.1016/j.acalib.2025.103041); [source](https://asistdl.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/pra2.1159?af=R)).
- **Critiques of the evaluator-only framing exist but are scattered.** Mike Caulfield, the originator of SIFT, argues in a 2024 AACE interview that "AI is less like programming and more like spreadsheets" and that the value lies in user domain expertise channeled through AI rather than AI-evaluation skills per se ([source](https://aace.org/review/caulfield-ai/)). The AILIS 1.0 framework (Montesi & Bornstein, JAL 2025) found LIS students overestimate their AI literacy and that "softer" ethics competencies are more developed than "harder" technical ones, hinting at a scale problem in evaluator framings ([source](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0099133325001144)).

### 1. Primary Association Documents (IFLA, ALA, ACRL)

#### 1.1 IFLA Statement on Libraries and Artificial Intelligence (2020)

The IFLA Statement was agreed by the IFLA Governing Board on September 17, 2020 and was authored under the IFLA FAIFE (Committee on Freedom of Access to Information and Freedom of Expression) ([source](https://repository.ifla.org/bitstreams/8d21df6c-e61b-41a5-b089-0353a5552dbd/download)). Although three years older than the ChatGPT moment, it is the document most cited as the international precedent for library AI engagement, and it is striking how cleanly it anticipates the post-2022 generative-AI literature.

The statement directs libraries to "Help their patrons develop digital literacies that include an understanding of how AI and algorithms work, and corresponding privacy and ethics questions" ([source](https://repository.ifla.org/bitstreams/8d21df6c-e61b-41a5-b089-0353a5552dbd/download)). It directs library associations to "Support library professionals to understand the impact of AI as well as its intersections with privacy and ethical principles" and to ensure "librarians are able to develop relevant digital skills and competencies" ([source](https://repository.ifla.org/bitstreams/8d21df6c-e61b-41a5-b089-0353a5552dbd/download)).

Annexure ii of the statement defines AI literacy as a four-part construct: "(1) A basic understanding of how AI and ML work, their underlying logic and their limitations; (2) Understanding the potential societal impacts of AI, especially in the area of human rights; (3) Personal data management skills; (4) Media and Information literacy" ([source](https://repository.ifla.org/bitstreams/8d21df6c-e61b-41a5-b089-0353a5552dbd/download)). This is the most-cited definition in subsequent LIS scholarship and is the structural ancestor of both the ACRL 2025 Competencies and the EDUCAUSE AI Literacy Framework.

Notably, IFLA's framing is already a hybrid: it positions libraries as deliverers of literacy (an evaluator-adjacent role) but also explicitly as actors in AI procurement, ethical research support, and public policy. The statement instructs librarians to "lend their expertise in data storage and licensing, data quality assessment, and safe and ethical information storage to help researchers address some of the concerns around data" ([source](https://repository.ifla.org/bitstreams/8d21df6c-e61b-41a5-b089-0353a5552dbd/download)). This is meaningfully broader than "evaluate the output."

**Confidence: High.** Direct quotation from the primary document.

#### 1.2 ALA Center for the Future of Libraries: AI Trend Page

The ALA Center for the Future of Libraries maintains an "Artificial Intelligence" trend page that frames libraries as workforce-development hubs in an AI economy and as locations to advance "algorithmic literacy while also ensuring an equitable and inclusive future" (citing the Urban Libraries Council) ([source](https://www.ala.org/future/trends/artificialintelligence)). The page is undated and is a synthesis rather than a policy document, but it is frequently cited in LIS LibGuides as ALA's public-facing AI position pre-2026.

**Confidence: Medium.** Page lacks an explicit publication date.

#### 1.3 ALA Guidance on the Use of AI in Libraries (April 2026 Draft)

The ALA Artificial Intelligence Policy Working Group published a public-comment draft titled "American Library Association: Guidance on the Use of Artificial Intelligence in Libraries" on April 24, 2026, with comments due May 8, 2026 ([source](https://acrl.ala.org/acrlinsider/ala-draft-guidance-of-the-use-of-artificial-intelligence-in-libraries-comments-due-may-8/)). The Working Group is co-chaired by Chip McAuley and Ed Sanchez and includes representatives from ACRL (six members), CORE, IFRT, IFC, RUSA, PLA, GNCRT, IRRT, NMRT, SustainRT, and CALA, with section leads from the IFC, ACRL, CORE, and the Public Policy & Advocacy Office ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2026-04/AI%20Working%20Group%20-%20%20DRAFT%20for%20ALA%20Board%20Council%20and%20Members.pdf)).

The draft is structured around five ALA Core Values (Public Good, Intellectual Freedom, Privacy, Sustainability, DEIA) and an appendix Ethics Framework derived from Luciano Floridi's work. Each value section has a "Principle + In Practice" essay followed by area-of-the-library implications (Collections, Public Services, Technical Services, Administration). The Working Group writes that "AI guidance should build on existing ALA policies rather than operate as a separate or conflicting layer" ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2026-04/AI%20Working%20Group%20-%20%20DRAFT%20for%20ALA%20Board%20Council%20and%20Members.pdf)).

For an IJoL lit review, the draft's most-citable framings are:

- "Libraries will educate the public about AI's benefits, risks, and ethical and non-ethical uses through programming and resources guided by an AI Literacy Framework. Instruction and reference will use a harm-reduction lens, helping patrons recognize risks such as misinformation, privacy threats, and mental health impacts" ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2026-04/AI%20Working%20Group%20-%20%20DRAFT%20for%20ALA%20Board%20Council%20and%20Members.pdf)).
- The intellectual-freedom section directs that libraries should be "Treating AI outputs as drafts that must be critically evaluated and edited by humans, especially for complex reference, local context, or high-stakes topics" ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2026-04/AI%20Working%20Group%20-%20%20DRAFT%20for%20ALA%20Board%20Council%20and%20Members.pdf)).
- "Libraries will provide programming and reference services to help patrons develop critical evaluation skills for AI-generated content, including understanding how AI tools may reflect or challenge social biases and how reliance on AI can affect human critical thinking and information literacy" ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2026-04/AI%20Working%20Group%20-%20%20DRAFT%20for%20ALA%20Board%20Council%20and%20Members.pdf)).
- The Public Good section pre-commits to a forthcoming "AI Literacy Framework" but does not provide it: this is a gap an IJoL paper can fill.

The draft does **not** explicitly cite the ACRL Framework for Information Literacy or the 2025 ACRL AI Competencies, even though six Working Group members are ACRL-affiliated. This is itself worth noting: the ALA-level statement positions evaluation/instruction as a core function but does not yet anchor the pedagogy to the ACRL frames.

**Confidence: High.** Direct quotation from the public-comment draft.

#### 1.4 ACRL AI Competencies for Academic Library Workers (October 2025)

This is the document most directly relevant to the IJoL paper's audience. The "AI Competencies for Academic Library Workers" was approved by the ACRL Board of Directors in October 2025 (the press conference of approval was October 3, 2025) ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2025-10/acrl_ai_competencies.pdf)). It was developed by a 15-member task force chaired by Jason Coleman and Keven Jeffery, with Dr. Leo S. Lo (the framework's intellectual progenitor) as ACRL Board liaison ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2025-10/acrl_ai_competencies.pdf)).

The framing is the key item to cite for the IJoL paper. The Competencies document writes:

> "In developing these competencies, the committee recognized significant parallels between responsible AI use and the principles of critical information literacy, as outlined in documents like ACRL's Framework for Information Literacy for Higher Education. The ability to understand how knowledge is produced, valued, and ethically applied is as essential when working with AI tools as it is when using tools based on other technologies. For example, AI-generated content presents many of the same challenges that information professionals have long addressed: bias, inaccuracies, and questions of authority and credibility. These parallels reinforce librarians' established role in advocating for informed and ethical information practices, now extended to include AI literacy in both personal and professional contexts." ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2025-10/acrl_ai_competencies.pdf))

This is the canonical statement of the "AI literacy is information literacy extended" position the IJoL paper will engage with.

The Competencies are organized as **5 Mindsets** (Curiosity, Skepticism, Judgment, Responsibility, Collaboration) and **18 Competencies in 4 Categories**:
- **1. Ethical Considerations** (5): equitable access, fairness, privacy/IP, accountability, broader impacts.
- **2. Knowledge & Understanding** (5): basic AI tech, attribution/detection, staying current, source evaluation, policies/regulations.
- **3. Analysis & Evaluation** (4): explaining AI impacts, evaluating benefits/risks, multifaceted analysis, task-context evaluation.
- **4. Use & Application** (5): task efficiency, communication/collaboration, prompting, innovation, accessibility/usability ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2025-10/acrl_ai_competencies.pdf)).

For an IJoL argument that wants to push beyond evaluation, two specific competencies are useful targets:

- **2.4 ("Critically evaluate sources of information about AI for accuracy and bias")** explicitly tells library workers to "Apply information literacy skills and evaluation frameworks to analyze sources, claims, arguments, and language in AI-related information." This is the document explicitly mapping IL evaluation onto AI outputs ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2025-10/acrl_ai_competencies.pdf)).
- **3.3 ("Critically analyze AI technologies using a multifaceted approach")** directs analysts to "Consider technical capabilities of AI technologies, including accuracy, relevance, and robustness of their performance" and "ethical aspects of AI technologies, including transparency, explainability, biases and fairness" ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2025-10/acrl_ai_competencies.pdf)).

What the document **does not** explicitly do: it does not articulate the *constraint pattern* (epistemic discipline as the practitioner skill of bounding what AI is asked to do, rather than evaluating what it produces). The closest gesture is competency 4.3 on prompting strategies, but prompting is treated as task optimization, not as constraint-pattern epistemic work. This is the wedge the IJoL paper can use.

The Competencies cite **eight references** in their notes; two are foundational for any literature review: Lo's "AI Literacy: A Guide for Academic Libraries" (2025) and Ng et al.'s "Conceptualizing AI literacy: An exploratory review" (Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence, 2021), which provided the four-category structure ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2025-10/acrl_ai_competencies.pdf)).

**Confidence: High.** Direct quotation from the primary document.

#### 1.5 ACRL Framework Sandbox: AI Topic

ACRL maintains a Framework Sandbox (sandbox.acrl.org) where librarians submit OER-style activities mapped to the Framework's six frames. The "Artificial Intelligence" topic page hosts at least 10 AI-focused teaching resources, mapping to five of six frames: Authority is Constructed and Contextual; Information Creation as Process; Information Has Value; Searching as Strategic Exploration; and Research as Inquiry. "Scholarship as Conversation" is notably absent from the indexed AI resources ([source](https://sandbox.acrl.org/topics/artificial-intelligence-ai)). The single most-viewed resource is "Generative AI in Academic Life: Learning, Evaluating, and Prompting with Purpose" (3,286 views) ([source](https://sandbox.acrl.org/topics/artificial-intelligence-ai)).

This is empirical evidence that field-level AI-literacy practice is *already* mapping AI onto the existing IL frames, which strengthens the case that an IJoL paper challenging the evaluator-extension model has a real and active audience.

#### 1.6 ACRL Instruction Section: "Tips and Trends" on AI (April 2024)

Justine Martin and Matt Armstrong (ACRL IS Instructional Technologies Committee) published "Tips and Trends: AI Developments and Resources for Academic Librarians" on April 12, 2024 ([source](https://acrl.ala.org/IS/tips-and-trends-ai-developments-and-resources-for-academic-librarians/)). The article frames librarians as uniquely qualified intermediaries: "instruction and reference librarians… have an opportunity to guide university communities through ethical adoption of AI tools" ([source](https://acrl.ala.org/IS/tips-and-trends-ai-developments-and-resources-for-academic-librarians/)). The piece explicitly directs readers to the ACRL Framework Sandbox for AI integration with the IL Framework.

#### 1.7 ARL Research Libraries Guiding Principles for AI (April 2024)

The Association of Research Libraries published "Research Libraries Guiding Principles for Artificial Intelligence" in April 2024. One principle directly maps to AI literacy: "Libraries democratize access to artificial intelligence tools and technology to foster digital literacy among all people. Libraries embrace the opportunity to educate library users and information professionals about AI, equipping them with the knowledge and skills needed to thrive in a society increasingly reliant on artificial intelligence" ([source](https://www.arl.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/Research-Libraries-Guiding-Principles-for-Artificial-Intelligence.pdf)). This is the research-library-side complement to the public/academic statements above.

### 2. The ACRL Framework for Information Literacy and Its AI Revision

The 2016 ACRL "Framework for Information Literacy for Higher Education" is the underlying conceptual structure that nearly every academic-library AI-literacy paper invokes. Its six threshold-concept frames are: Authority is Constructed and Contextual; Information Creation as Process; Information Has Value; Research as Inquiry; Scholarship as Conversation; Searching as Strategic Exploration ([source](https://www.ala.org/acrl/standards/ilframework)).

In 2023 the ACRL Board chartered a Framework Review and Revision Task Force, and the task force has used C&RL News to communicate its working position. The most useful citation for an IJoL lit review is Miller, Ross, and Maye (2026) in C&RL News:

> "the Framework is robust and remains relevant today and will continue to be applicable well into the future" ([source](https://crln.acrl.org/index.php/crlnews/article/view/27281/35079)).

The task force position is that AI does not require new frames; rather, "AI and emerging technologies, critical practice, sustainability, and civil discourse" will be addressed by adding "references and examples of things like AI and critical/reflective pedagogies into the existing Framework language" ([source](https://crln.acrl.org/index.php/crlnews/article/view/27281/35079)).

**This is important for the IJoL paper.** The official ACRL position is that AI literacy is a special case of existing information literacy. An argument that the librarian's distinctive contribution to AI literacy is *teaching the constraint pattern* (epistemic discipline as practitioner skill) is *not* in tension with this revision direction, but it does require the IJoL paper to argue that the existing frames do not yet articulate constraint-pattern teaching. The closest existing frame is "Information Creation as Process" (which conceptualizes information as iteratively produced), but as currently written it focuses on *understanding* the process rather than *imposing* a process on AI generation.

### 3. Academic Library AI Literacy Curricula and LibGuides Scholarship

#### 3.1 LaFlamme (2025), Journal of Academic Librarianship — "Scaffolding AI literacy"

Katherine A. LaFlamme. (2025). Scaffolding AI literacy: An instructional model for academic librarianship. *Journal of Academic Librarianship*, 51(3), 103041. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.acalib.2025.103041 ([source](https://colab.ws/articles/10.1016/j.acalib.2025.103041)).

LaFlamme argues that "Academic librarians, with their expertise in information literacy and critical pedagogy, are well-equipped to address this need" for AI literacy. The model is a four-tier scaffold: Tier 1 foundational awareness; Tier 2 applied problem-solving; Tier 3 critical evaluation of AI-generated content; Tier 4 ethical advocacy ([source](https://colab.ws/articles/10.1016/j.acalib.2025.103041)). Tier 3 is where the evaluator framing lives: it positions students as "critical consumers of AI technologies" ([source](https://colab.ws/articles/10.1016/j.acalib.2025.103041)).

This is the canonical 2025 JAL article for an IJoL lit review. It is also the cleanest example of the pattern the IJoL paper is critiquing: librarians extending IL pedagogy to evaluation of AI outputs.

**Confidence: High** for citation/structure; **Medium** for verbatim quotes (extracted via colab.ws abstract page rather than the publisher's full text).

#### 3.2 Ko et al. (2024), JASIST Proceedings — Library Guide Analysis

Ko, J., Liu, Y., et al. (2024). How Can Academic Librarians Support Generative AI Literacy: An Analysis of Library Guides Using the ACRL Information Literacy Framework. *Proceedings of the Association for Information Science and Technology*, 61. https://doi.org/10.1002/pra2.1159 ([source](https://asistdl.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/pra2.1159?af=R)).

The poster analyzed AI LibGuides at 28 ACRL award-winning libraries. The abstract states: "Using the ACRL 'Framework for Information Literacy for Higher Education,' we analyzed newly created guides from 28 libraries. We found that this framework could serve as a foundation for AI literacy" ([source](https://asistdl.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/pra2.1159?af=R)). This is empirical confirmation that the field-level practice maps to the ACRL frames; it is a more useful citation than narrative essays because it shows uptake.

#### 3.3 Hervieux & Wheatley (2024) — Choice White Paper

Hervieux, S. & Wheatley, A. (2024). *Building an AI Literacy Framework: Perspectives from Instruction Librarians and Current Information Literacy Tools*. White Paper, Choice (underwritten by Taylor & Francis) ([source](https://www.choice360.org/research/white-paper-building-an-ai-literacy-framework-perspectives-from-instruction-librarians-and-current-information-literacy-tools/)).

The white paper observes: "information literacy frameworks and standards have traditionally helped librarians plan their instruction sessions, [but] these structures do not address AI" ([source](https://www.choice360.org/research/white-paper-building-an-ai-literacy-framework-perspectives-from-instruction-librarians-and-current-information-literacy-tools/)). Hervieux and Wheatley's framework is the structural source for the ACRL 2025 Competencies (cited in the Competencies' Note 7) ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2025-10/acrl_ai_competencies.pdf)).

#### 3.4 Lo (2025), College & Research Libraries News — "AI Literacy: A Guide for Academic Libraries"

Lo, L. S. (2025). AI literacy: A guide for academic libraries. *College & Research Libraries News*, 86(3), 120. https://doi.org/10.5860/crln.86.3.120 ([source](https://digitalrepository.unm.edu/ulls_fsp/210)).

Lo defines AI literacy through five dimensions: technical knowledge, ethical awareness, critical thinking, practical skills, and societal impact ([source](https://digitalrepository.unm.edu/ulls_fsp/210)). Lo is the intellectual progenitor of the ACRL 2025 Competencies (he was Board liaison to the task force) and his AACE-style framing—"libraries should champion AI literacy as they historically supported print and digital literacy"—is the most-cited articulation of the librarian-as-AI-literacy-leader thesis ([source](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/student-success/academic-life/2025/02/10/how-librarians-can-engage-generative-ai)).

#### 3.5 Reshaping IL Programs in the Advent of ChatGPT (Internet Reference Services Quarterly)

Internet Reference Services Quarterly, 29(1), 2024 published "Reshaping Academic Library Information Literacy Programs in the Advent of ChatGPT and Other Generative AI Technologies" ([source](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10875301.2024.2400132)). The article is a useful counterpoint to LaFlamme's scaffold: it is more programmatic (about IL programs) than pedagogical (about individual instruction).

#### 3.6 AILIS 1.0 (Montesi & Bornstein 2025, JAL)

Montesi, M., & Bornstein, M. C. (2025). AILIS 1.0: A new framework to measure AI literacy in library and information science (LIS). *Journal of Academic Librarianship* ([source](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0099133325001144)). This is the first published assessment instrument for AI literacy specifically in LIS contexts. Empirical findings: in a sample of 100 LIS students and 63 academic librarians at the Complutense University of Madrid, "library professionals outperformed students, particularly in Ethics and Usage. However, students, especially first-years, reported higher self-efficacy despite lower performance, indicating a tendency to overestimate their AI literacy" ([source](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0099133325001144)).

Two implications for the IJoL paper: (1) there is a now-validated instrument to cite, and (2) the soft-vs-hard finding (ethics competencies stronger than technical) supports an argument that the field's current pedagogy under-trains the technical/constraint-pattern dimension.

#### 3.7 Ali & Richardson (2025) — Scoping Review

Ali, M. Y., & Richardson, J. (2025). AI literacy guidelines and policies for academic libraries: A scoping review. *IFLA Journal*, https://doi.org/10.1177/03400352251321192 ([source](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/03400352251321192)). ### 4. The Librarian-as-Evaluator Framing: SIFT, CRAAP, and Their Extensions to AI

The dominant LibGuide-level pedagogy for evaluating AI outputs is to adapt Mike Caulfield's SIFT method (Stop; Investigate the source; Find better coverage; Trace claims to original) to generative AI. The University of Michigan-Dearborn guide is representative:

- **Stop**: "Is this a good use case for an LLM?"
- **Investigate**: most LLMs do not cite sources; when they do, citations may be fabricated.
- **Find**: "for many topics, there may be a better source than UM-GPT or other Generative AI tool."
- **Trace**: since AI outputs often lack sources, users must independently verify claims ([source](https://guides.umd.umich.edu/c.php?g=1399575&p=10353762)).

This pattern is replicated at UChicago, Queen's University, UBC, U Alberta, USMA, Spokane CC, and Gateway Tech College, among many others. The empirical claim that "this framework could serve as a foundation for AI literacy" (Ko et al. 2024) is essentially a finding that LibGuide authors are doing this kind of SIFT-extension work.

CRAAP (Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose) is the older instrument, and it appears in older guides; it is less well-suited to AI outputs because Authority and Currency become incoherent for generated text. The post-2023 trend has been toward SIFT.

### 5. Critiques and Limits of the Evaluator Framing

This is the most important section for the IJoL paper because it is the thinnest part of the literature. Critiques exist but are scattered.

#### 5.1 Caulfield's Own Pivot

Mike Caulfield, the originator of SIFT, has explicitly distanced his framework from "AI evaluation" framings. In an AACE interview, Caulfield argues:

- **AI is "less like programming and more like spreadsheets"** — the value is in what the user brings to the tool, not in AI-evaluation skills. Students "need deep specialized knowledge and theoretical frameworks that AI helps implement—not AI proficiency itself" ([source](https://aace.org/review/caulfield-ai/)).
- Caulfield, when assessing custom AI tools, applies the same source-evaluation principles used for any information source. He stresses the importance of knowing whether creators have genuine expertise and "someone careful with the truth who rigorously tests their work" ([source](https://aace.org/review/caulfield-ai/)).
- Caulfield extends rather than rejects existing evaluation frameworks: SIFT principles "apply equally to assessing AI tools themselves" ([source](https://aace.org/review/caulfield-ai/)).

Caulfield's pivot is the single most useful citation for an IJoL argument that evaluation-only framings underspecify the practitioner skill. He is the originator of the most-cited evaluator framework and he has explicitly moved past it for AI.

#### 5.2 Scale Critique (LLRX, AI Law Librarians, AILIS findings)

A 2025 LLRX article ("How to Spot AI Hallucinations Like a Reference Librarian") and AI Law Librarians' "What the Science Says About Hallucinations in Legal Research" (Feb 2026) document that academic and law librarians "have observed a significant increase in workload related to verifying the accuracy of references" and that "about 40% of AI citations fail this basic test" of author-institution verification ([source](https://www.llrx.com/2025/12/how-to-spot-ai-hallucinations-like-a-reference-librarian/)). The scaling problem—librarians cannot evaluate every AI output for every patron—is the implicit critique of the evaluator-only framing.

The AILIS 1.0 finding that LIS students score higher on ethics competencies than on technical AI competencies, despite overestimating their literacy, is corroborating evidence that an evaluator-framed pedagogy produces students who can articulate ethics but cannot reliably catch hallucinations ([source](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0099133325001144)).

#### 5.3 Implicit Critique in the ALA Draft

The ALA Working Group draft says librarians should help patrons recognize "how reliance on AI can affect human critical thinking and information literacy" ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2026-04/AI%20Working%20Group%20-%20%20DRAFT%20for%20ALA%20Board%20Council%20and%20Members.pdf)). This is a gesture toward what the IJoL paper calls *epistemic discipline*—but the draft does not develop it into pedagogy.

### 6. The Constraint-Pattern Gap: What the Existing Conversation Does Not Cover

This section is for the IJoL paper's "gap" framing. Across the documents reviewed, no association statement, scholarly framework, or instructional model treats the constraint pattern (the epistemic discipline of bounding what an AI is asked to do, before any output is produced, in ways that prevent fabrication) as the librarian's distinctive teaching contribution.

The closest gestures:

- ACRL AI Competency 4.3 ("Develop effective prompting strategies for optimal AI output") treats prompting as task-optimization, not as epistemic constraint ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2025-10/acrl_ai_competencies.pdf)).
- Caulfield's argument that AI value comes from "what users bring to the technology, not from AI skills alone" ([source](https://aace.org/review/caulfield-ai/)) gestures at a constraint orientation but stops short of articulating epistemic discipline as a pedagogy.
- The ALA draft's reference to "harm-reduction lens" gestures toward constraint but does not specify it ([source](https://www.ala.org/sites/default/files/2026-04/AI%20Working%20Group%20-%20%20DRAFT%20for%20ALA%20Board%20Council%20and%20Members.pdf)).

The IJoL paper can plausibly claim that *teaching the constraint pattern* — i.e., teaching students to specify the epistemic conditions under which an AI is allowed to generate, before generation — is a contribution that the field's frameworks have not yet articulated, and that this contribution is more durable than evaluator skills because it does not require the librarian to re-evaluate every output.

**Confidence: High** that this gap exists in the documents reviewed; **Medium** that no other 2025-2026 paper has articulated it (full peer-reviewed literature is harder to fully canvass in a single research session, especially behind paywalls).
