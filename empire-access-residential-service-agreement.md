## TL;DR

Empire Access does not publish one single "Residential Internet Service Agreement." A residential customer is bound by a stack of four documents on `empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/` plus a per-customer Service Contract: the **Acceptable Use Policy** (last revised 11/18/13), the **Telecommunications Terms & Conditions**, the **Internet Service Level Agreement**, and the **Privacy Policy** (effective Jan 1, 2020). The marketing claim "no contracts" is partly true (AUP Section 14: customers without a Service Contract can cancel any time, in writing), but the Telecom T&C sets a default one-year initial term, automatic one-year renewals, and an early-termination fee equal to remaining monthly charges × months left, governed by New York law in Steuben County. No FCC Broadband Consumer Label was located on the public site, which is a likely compliance gap with FCC labeling rules in effect since April 2024.

## Current Status

- **Five binding documents identified**, all published on the Legal Resources hub. No consolidated Residential Subscriber Agreement exists. The AUP is dated 11/18/13.
- **Cancellation rights for no-contract residential customers are explicit** (AUP §14): cancel any time, in writing. This matches the homepage "no contracts" marketing claim, but only for customers who never signed a "Service Contract."
- **For anyone with a Service Contract**, the Telecom T&C is fully commercial-style: 1-year initial term, auto-renews for successive 1-year terms unless 30-day written non-renewal notice, ETF = remaining monthly charges × months remaining, governed by NY law in Steuben County.
- **No arbitration clause and no class-action waiver** were found in any document examined. Disputes go to NY state or federal courts in Steuben County.
- **No FCC Broadband Consumer Label was located** on the site or via search. The FCC has required these at point of sale since April 2024 for providers with 100,000+ subscribers and October 2024 for smaller providers. Empire Access falls under the rule.
- **Privacy Policy is light on modern frameworks.** Effective date is January 1, 2020. No explicit CCPA, CPRA, NY SHIELD Act, or specific retention timelines. CPNI is covered (federal telecom rule) and Empire claims it does not sell personal info.
- **No data caps, throttling, or formal Network Management Disclosure** were found. The AUP says only "EMPIRE ACCESS holds the right to manage any network abuse appropriately." This does not satisfy the FCC's network-management-disclosure requirements as published.

## Contract Structure

**Parties.** Per the Telecommunications T&C opening clause: "These Terms and Conditions of Service ('Terms and Conditions') apply to all telecommunications and related services (the 'Services') provided by **Empire Access Corporation or its affiliates** ('Empire Access') to its customer or its affiliates ('Customer') under the Service Contract into which these Terms and Conditions are incorporated." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/telecommunications-terms-conditions/))

The parent corporation referenced in the AUP indemnification clause is "Empire Tel. Corp." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/acceptable-use-policy/))

The Privacy Policy adds Empire Telephone Corporation as the affiliated New York corporation covering `empiretelephone.com`. ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/privacy-policy/))

**Form of acceptance.** The AUP states: "PLEASE READ CAREFULLY EMPIRE ACCESS provides its Customers with access to the Internet. By subscribing to our Service as a Customer, or by signing a Service Application, or by signing a Service Contract constitutes acceptance of the Acceptable Use Policy and Terms and Conditions of Service." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/acceptable-use-policy/))

In plain language: three acceptance paths, any of which binds the customer to both the AUP and the Telecom T&C. (1) subscribing to the service, (2) signing a Service Application (the online or paper signup form), (3) signing a Service Contract (a separate document negotiated with Empire, more common for business or bundled service customers).

**Term length and auto-renewal.** The default term per Telecom T&C: "Unless the Service Contract states otherwise, the term of the Service Contract commences upon Acceptance Date of the Services and continues for the time period indicated in the Service Contract... At expiration, the Term will automatically renew for successive renewal terms of one (1) year each, unless either Empire Access or Customer gives written notice to the other of its election not to renew the Term at least thirty (30) days before the expiration of the then-current Term." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/telecommunications-terms-conditions/))

In the Telecom T&C's own consumer-summary footer, Empire restates this: "Services begin upon the Acceptance Date for an **initial one-year term** and automatically renew for successive one-year periods." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/telecommunications-terms-conditions/))

**Critical interpretation issue.** The Telecom T&C is written as if every customer has a "Service Contract" with a stated term. Residential customers who sign up online via `shop.empireaccess.com` likely do NOT sign a separate Service Contract document; they accept terms via a click-through Service Application. AUP §14 expressly recognizes this: "Customers **without a Service Contract** may cancel their Service, in writing, at any time. Customers who signed a Service Contract must abide by the terms of the Service Contract." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/acceptable-use-policy/))

So whether the one-year-term + ETF regime in the Telecom T&C applies to a given residential customer depends entirely on whether their signup flow generated a "Service Contract" or just a "Service Application." This is not made clear on the public site and is the single biggest ambiguity in the residential agreement stack. (Confidence: High that the ambiguity exists; Medium on which path the default residential online order takes. The homepage's "no contracts" claim implies Service Application, but the only way to verify is to walk through the actual checkout.)

## Key Terms: Customer-Facing Obligations

### Service term / contract length

**Marketing claim:** Empire's residential plan pages state "**No contracts or hidden fees**" on all three tiers (Essential 500 Mbps, Enhanced 1 Gig, Elite 2 Gig). ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/residential/))

**AUP §14 (verbatim):** "Customers without a Service Contract may cancel their Service, in writing, at any time. Customers who signed a Service Contract must abide by the terms of the Service Contract." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/acceptable-use-policy/))

**Telecom T&C verbatim:** "the term of the Service Contract commences upon Acceptance Date of the Services and continues for the time period indicated in the Service Contract... At expiration, the Term will automatically renew for successive renewal terms of one (1) year each, unless either Empire Access or Customer gives written notice to the other of its election not to renew the Term at least thirty (30) days before the expiration of the then-current Term." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/telecommunications-terms-conditions/))

(Confidence: High on the verbatim quotes. Medium on what happens at residential online checkout. See Open Questions.)

### Early termination fee (ETF)

**Telecom T&C Termination Liability clause (verbatim):** "If the Service Contract is terminated before expiration of the Term, then except as provided in the next sentence, Customer shall pay to Empire Access, immediately upon demand, (a) all sums then due and owing under the Service Contract, (b. Commercial) **an amount equal to the recurring Monthly Charges (as listed in the Service Contract) multiplied by the number of months left in the Term.** Despite the preceding sentence, Customer will not be liable for termination liability in the case of (a) non-renewal of the Term by Empire Access or Customer in accordance with section 1 of these Terms and Conditions, or (b) permitted termination by Customer under the SLA, but in either such case Customer shall pay all charges incurred prior to the effective date of expiration or non-renewal of the Service Contract." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/telecommunications-terms-conditions/))

**Plain English:** If you have a Service Contract and you cancel before the term expires, you owe the rest of the term in full. On a 1-year initial term at, say, $80/mo, canceling at month 3 would be a 9 × $80 = $720 charge. There are two exits: (1) wait until the 30-day non-renewal window before term expiration and give written notice, or (2) terminate under the SLA's chronic-outage right (see SLA section below).

**AUP §14 exit for non-Service-Contract customers:** "Customers without a Service Contract may cancel their Service, in writing, at any time." No ETF. ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/acceptable-use-policy/))

### Automatic renewal

Telecom T&C: 1-year auto-renewals, 30 days written notice required to opt out. ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/telecommunications-terms-conditions/))

For residential subscribers without a Service Contract, there is no annual auto-renewal mechanic. Service continues month to month, and the customer can cancel in writing at any time (AUP §14). ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/acceptable-use-policy/))

### Pricing and price changes

**AUP §15 (verbatim):** "EMPIRE ACCESS reserves the right to change the rates and otherwise modify the terms and conditions of this Agreement by notifying Customer 10 days in advance of the effective date of such proposed changes. **If Customer does not request its Service to be terminated, it will be conclusively presumed that Customer consents to the new terms conditions, and rates as so notified.**" ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/acceptable-use-policy/))

This is the most consequential price-change clause in the agreement stack. Translation: Empire can raise prices on 10 days notice, and silence equals consent. This applies to all residential subscribers under the AUP. The Telecom T&C is silent on mid-term price increases.

For the **Security & Automation** services (not core internet but useful for comparison), Empire is more restrictive on itself: "Rates and charges may increase once per calendar year." That cap does not appear in the AUP or Telecom T&C governing internet. ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/security-and-automation-terms-conditions/))

### Billing, late fees, payment disputes

**Telecom T&C:** "All bills are due and payable upon receipt. If Customer does not pay the amount due within 30 days of the invoice date, Customer shall pay a **late fee of 1.5% per month** on the unpaid balance." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/telecommunications-terms-conditions/))

The AUP §13 adds: "EMPIRE ACCESS has the right to charge applicable fees for any returned checks." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/acceptable-use-policy/))

AUP §20: "EMPIRE ACCESS reserves the right to bill a reconnect fee for services after termination for non payment." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/acceptable-use-policy/))

The amount of the reconnect fee and returned-check fee are not published.

The current monthly promo terms (a separate document) disclose a non-AutoPay fee: "Pricing includes enrollment in AutoPay and paperless billing. Customers who do not enroll in AutoPay and/or paperless billing will incur a **$5/month fee for each**." That's potentially $10/mo for a customer who doesn't want autopay or paperless billing. [source: https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/telecommunications-terms-conditions/ (footer promo language)]

### Equipment (modem / router / ONT)

**Telecom T&C verbatim:** "Equipment and facilities furnished by Empire Access are and remain its property and Customer shall return all such equipment and facilities to Empire Access on expiration or termination of the Service Contract or as earlier requested by Empire Access, in good condition, reasonable wear and tear expected. Customer shall reimburse Empire Access for any loss of, or damage to, Empire Access's equipment or facilities on the Customer's premises, except loss or damage caused by Empire Access's own employees, agents, or contractors." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/telecommunications-terms-conditions/))

Empire's residential pricing pages confirm equipment is included with all three plans: "Wi-Fi router and modem" included as standard, plus "FREE professional installation." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/residential/))

**Practical implications:** The Wi-Fi router and modem (and ONT, by extension) are leased, not owned. They must be returned at termination. Damage charges apply but the schedule of damage charges is not published. No grace window for return is specified in the Telecom T&C.

### Data caps, throttling, network management

**No data caps were found in any document.** This is consistent with Empire's residential marketing, which advertises symmetric service without usage limits.

**Throttling / network management:** The only relevant clause is AUP §21: "**EMPIRE ACCESS holds the right to manage any network abuse appropriately.**" ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/acceptable-use-policy/))

There is no formal Network Management Disclosure document on the legal-resources hub. FCC Open Internet transparency rules require ISPs to publish a network management disclosure with specific performance characteristics, commercial terms, and network practices. The single-sentence AUP §21 does not appear to meet that standard, and no separate disclosure document was located. (Confidence: High that no document was located; Medium on whether one exists somewhere on the site outside the legal-resources hub.)

### Acceptable Use Policy summary: what's prohibited, what triggers suspension

The AUP enumerates prohibited conduct. Direct quotes:

- **§5:** "EMPIRE ACCESS reserves the right to immediately terminate the service of any customer who jeopardizes the efficiency of the system by sending unsolicited mass e-mail, commercial or otherwise (SPAM) or posting commercial messages to inappropriate newsgroups." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/acceptable-use-policy/))
- **§8:** "The Service may only be used for lawful purposes... Trafficking of access codes, credit card numbers, social security numbers or similar information is unlawful. Any use by Customer of the Service for unlawful purposes will constitute grounds for EMPIRE ACCESS to discontinue Customer's Service." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/acceptable-use-policy/))
- **§10:** "The sharing of passwords or accounts is strictly prohibited and violators are subject to the cancellation of such Customer's Service without prior notice. **The resale of the Service or any other associated services by any and all means is restricted unless approved in advance in writing by EMPIRE ACCESS.**" ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/acceptable-use-policy/))
- **§11:** Single-user/household accounts only. "Sharing the account with persons other than family members residing in an individual Customer's household, whether for compensation or otherwise, is strictly prohibited." Plus: "The Internet Access Service cannot be shared between multiple dwellings or residences" (§1). ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/acceptable-use-policy/))
- **§16:** Don't introduce viruses. "Customer agrees not to introduce, knowingly or unknowingly, any virus onto the Internet system or EMPIRE ACCESS' hosts." (Note the "unknowingly" word, which is a strict-liability-style construction.) ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/acceptable-use-policy/))
- **§17 (notable):** Help desk abuse can trigger termination. "Abusive messages left in our voice mail or abusive conduct when speaking to an EMPIRE ACCESS help desk technician MAY result in a termination of service." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/acceptable-use-policy/))

**Consequences ladder:**
- For SPAM, illegal use, password sharing, virus introduction, privacy violations of other users, and help-desk abuse: **immediate termination** at Empire's discretion.
- For other violations: termination "upon any violation by Customer of any of the terms and conditions" or "upon 30 days prior written notice" (AUP §14).

There is no formal escalation procedure, written warning requirement, or appeal process disclosed in the AUP.

### Service-level commitments from Empire (uptime, install windows, outage credits)

**Internet SLA verbatim:** "Empire Access is committed to providing **99.99% Service Availability**, defined as a network connection that is connected and exchanging IP packets with the Empire Access network and the internet." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/internet-service-level-agreement/))

**Outage credit schedule (verbatim from SLA):**

| Length of Service non-Availability | Credit |
|---|---|
| 120 minutes to 240 minutes | 10% of MRC |
| 240 minutes to 480 minutes | 20% of MRC |
| 480 minutes to 960 minutes | 30% of MRC |
| 960 minutes to 1,920 minutes | 40% of MRC |
| Over 1,920 minutes | 50% of MRC |

([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/internet-service-level-agreement/))

**Latency standard:** "less than 60 ms in North America and less than 160 ms elsewhere throughout the world." Violation = 10% MRC credit. ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/internet-service-level-agreement/))

**Packet delivery standard:** ≥99.9% monthly average. Violation = 10% MRC credit. Sustained ≤95% (NA) or ≤92% (global) for 4+ hours counts as full service unavailability. ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/internet-service-level-agreement/))

**Chronic outage termination right (verbatim):** "If an individual circuit experiences either: (i) a single period of Service Non-Availability in excess of twenty-four hours in any calendar month, (ii) three or more periods of Service Non-Availability of four hours or longer over the course of a rolling six month period, or (iii) five separate periods of Service Non-Availability (of any duration) within a calendar month, **Customer may terminate the services without penalty** provided that written notice of termination is provided to Empire Access within ninety days of the event giving rise to the termination right." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/internet-service-level-agreement/))

**Important catches in the SLA:**

1. **Credits are not automatic.** "SLA credits are applied only upon Customer written request, which must be submitted within thirty (30) calendar days of the end of the month in which entitlement of an SLA credit arose." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/internet-service-level-agreement/)) If a customer doesn't notice and ask within 30 days of the month end, the credit is forfeited.
2. **Outage clock starts when the trouble ticket is opened**, not when the outage actually began. "Service non-Availability must be reported by the customer by opening a trouble ticket and the start of the Service non-Availability starts when the trouble ticket is opened." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/internet-service-level-agreement/)) An outage that the customer doesn't notice (e.g., overnight) for 6 hours and then reports at hour 7 is only counted as a 1-hour outage for SLA purposes, which falls below the 120-minute credit threshold.
3. **Maximum monthly credit is 100% of MRC.** "In no event will SLA credits in any calendar month exceed 100% of the total MRC payable by Customer for the Service in that month." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/internet-service-level-agreement/))
4. **No install-window commitment.** The SLA does not commit Empire to any installation timeline, missed-appointment credit, or service-activation window.

## Key Terms: Dispute Resolution

### Arbitration / class action

**No mandatory arbitration clause was found in the AUP, Telecom T&C, SLA, Privacy Policy, or Refund Policy.** No class-action waiver was found. No opt-out window or governing-law-for-arbitration provisions exist because there is no arbitration regime to opt out of. This is a customer-favorable departure from the boilerplate of most large ISPs (Spectrum, Verizon, Frontier, etc., all of which impose arbitration on residential customers). (Confidence: High based on the full text of all four core documents.)

### Governing law

**Telecom T&C verbatim:** "The Contract Documents will be governed by and construed in accordance with the laws of the State of New York without regard to principles of conflicts of laws. The parties each hereby consent to personal jurisdiction in the state and federal courts in **Steuben County, New York**." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/telecommunications-terms-conditions/))

**AUP §19 verbatim:** "This agreement is made under and shall be governed by and construed in accordance with the laws of, the State of New York applicable to agreements made and performed in New York. **Any cause of action of Customer or its designated users with respect to the Service must be instituted within one year after the claim or cause of action has arisen or it will be barred.**" ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/acceptable-use-policy/))

The one-year limitations clause is significant: NY's default statute of limitations for breach of contract is six years (CPLR 213). The AUP shortens that to one year for any claim arising out of the Service. For a customer in Ithaca (Tompkins County), this means traveling to Steuben County to litigate. Bath, NY is the county seat, roughly 90 minutes by road from Ithaca.

### Limitation of liability

**Telecom T&C verbatim:** "IN NO EVENT SHALL EMPIRE ACCESS'S CUMULATIVE LIABILITY FOR ALL CLAIMS ARISING OUT OF THE SERVICE CONTRACT DOCUMENTS EXCEED THE TOTAL AMOUNT OF ALL FEES PAID BY CUSTOMER TO EMPIRE ACCESS THEREUNDER." ([source](https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/telecommunications-terms-conditions/))

The exclusive remedies tier (verbatim):
- "FOR A SERVICE QUALITY CLAIM (INCLUDING INTERRUPTION IN SERVICE), THE OUTAGE CREDIT UNDER THE SLA"

## Differences vs. Industry Norms

This section compares Empire Access's residential terms against the typical residential ISP boilerplate (Spectrum/Charter, Verizon Fios, Frontier, AT&T). Confidence: Medium, because the comparison summarizes general industry knowledge of those agreements rather than fresh fetches of each.

**Customer-favorable departures from norm:**

1. **No mandatory arbitration or class-action waiver.** This is a major departure from Spectrum, Verizon, Frontier, and AT&T, all of which require arbitration and waive class actions in their residential agreements. Empire Access customers retain full court access. (High confidence in the absence; Medium confidence in the comparison since no fresh competitor-agreement fetch was done.)
2. **No data caps and no usage-based throttling clauses.** AT&T's residential fiber has historically used soft caps; Spectrum has not throttled residential broadband but reserves the right under "reasonable network management." Empire's agreement only reserves the right to manage "network abuse."
3. **No-Service-Contract residential customers can cancel any time in writing without ETF** (AUP §14). Many large ISPs use term agreements aggressively to retain ETFs even where they market "no contract" promo tiers.
4. **Chronic outage termination right (24-hour single outage, 3× 4-hour outages over six months, or 5 outages of any duration in one month) is reasonably generous** compared to typical residential SLAs, which rarely give a termination right at all for residential service.

**Customer-unfavorable departures from norm:**

1. **10-day price-change notice with silence-as-consent (AUP §15) is aggressive.** Most residential ISPs give 30 days notice and explicitly state the customer can cancel without ETF if they object. Empire's AUP is dated 11/18/13, predating many state and FCC notice-period norms.
2. **The AUP is dated 2013.** Twelve years without a public revision date update suggests the residential subscriber agreement has not been refreshed to reflect modern FCC rules (broadband labels, network management transparency), state privacy laws (CCPA, NY SHIELD), or industry norm changes.
3. **SLA credits require customer written request within 30 days of month-end and don't auto-apply.** Many competitor SLAs auto-apply credit. Practical impact: most residential customers will never claim these credits.
4. **One-year shortened limitations period for legal claims (AUP §19)** is unusual against NY's six-year default for contract claims, and aggressive compared to standard industry agreements which typically leave the statutory period intact.
5. **Steuben County, NY exclusive jurisdiction** is geographically inconvenient for customers in Empire's other footprint areas (e.g., a customer in northern PA or in Tompkins County, NY).

**Areas where Empire is neither better nor worse than industry norm:**

- 1.5%/month late fee (industry standard).
- Equipment leased and must be returned (industry standard).
- Indemnification by customer (industry standard).
- Limitation of liability to fees paid (industry standard).
- AutoPay/paperless billing fee for opting out ($5 each, $10 combined) (becoming industry standard, though debatable on consumer-fairness grounds).

## Red Flags / Watch-Outs for Residential Customers

Before signing up with Empire Access residential fiber, a prospective customer should be aware of the following:

1. **"No contracts" marketing is conditional.** If the online checkout at `shop.empireaccess.com` generates only a Service Application (not a Service Contract), the customer can cancel any time in writing under AUP §14. But if any signup channel (door-to-door rep, phone sales, business-bundled offer, or promotion-bound offer) involves signing a Service Contract document, the Telecom T&C kicks in with a 1-year initial term, auto-renewing, with an ETF equal to monthly charges × remaining months. **Ask explicitly during signup: "Am I signing a Service Application or a Service Contract?"**

2. **Promo and gift-card terms can lock in conduct, even without a Service Contract.** The current "3 months free + $100 Walmart Gift Card" promo (SPRINGFIBER, May 2026) requires Enhanced or Elite tier and forfeits remaining free months if the customer downgrades. The "$200 Streaming Choice Card" promo requires 60 days of active service in good standing. These are not formal ETFs but they create economic friction against early cancellation. [source: https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/telecommunications-terms-conditions/ (promo footer)]

3. **$5/month each for opting out of AutoPay or paperless billing.** A customer who wants paper bills and to pay by check pays $10/month above the headline rate. [source: https://www.empireaccess.com/about-us/empire-fiber-legal-resources/telecommunications-terms-conditions/ (promo footer)]

4. **AUP §15: prices can rise on 10 days notice with silence = consent.** This is the single most consequential consumer-unfriendly clause in the entire stack. A customer who doesn't read every email from Empire could see a price increase quietly take effect.

5. **The AUP was last revised November 18, 2013.** This is the residential service agreement. A 12-year-old document doesn't reflect FCC broadband labels (April 2024), the modernized FCC Open Internet rules, NY SHIELD Act (2020), CCPA / CPRA, or other modern consumer-protection frameworks. The Privacy Policy is newer (Jan 1, 2020) but still pre-CPRA, and is silent on state-by-state rights.

6. **No FCC Broadband Consumer Label was found on the public site.** This means a prospective customer cannot easily comparison-shop the monthly price, fees, contract terms, data allowance, typical speeds, and one-time install fees in the standardized FCC-mandated format that competing ISPs are now required to publish. (The checkout flow at `shop.empireaccess.com` was not walked through end-to-end in unauthenticated form during this research; labels may appear at that step but were not found via public navigation.)

7. **No formal Network Management Disclosure was found.** AUP §21's single sentence ("EMPIRE ACCESS holds the right to manage any network abuse appropriately") does not appear to satisfy FCC Open Internet transparency rules. The practical impact for most customers is zero (Empire is a fiber overbuilder with abundant capacity and no announced caps), but the absence is a compliance question worth noting.

8. **SLA credits require active customer pursuit.** They don't auto-apply. If you have a multi-hour outage, you must open a trouble ticket (which is also what starts the SLA clock; outages that pre-date the ticket don't count), then submit a written credit request within 30 days of the month end. Most residential customers will never claim these credits.

9. **Shortened one-year limitations period for any cause of action against Empire (AUP §19).** NY default is six years for contract claims. Empire's clause cuts that to one. Customers should treat any service dispute as time-sensitive.

10. **Steuben County, NY is the agreed venue for any dispute.** For an Ithaca-based (Tompkins County) customer, that's roughly a 90-minute drive to Bath, NY. Filing in any other court risks a venue-transfer motion at customer expense.

11. **CPNI sharing is opt-out, not opt-in.** Empire shares your usage data (the amount, type, and destination of your service usage) with its affiliates, agents, and contractors for marketing by default. Opt-out requires a written paper notice mailed to Prattsburgh, NY.

12. **Empire Access equipment (router, modem, ONT) is leased.** Damage charges apply at termination; the schedule is not published.
