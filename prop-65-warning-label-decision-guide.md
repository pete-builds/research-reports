## Current Status / TL;DR

- **Prop 65 is a warning law, not a ban.** California tells businesses to either keep listed chemicals below a "safe harbor" threshold or post a warning. Most companies post the warning because it is cheaper than testing, reformulating, or defending a lawsuit. Confidence: **High**.
- **The warning alone tells you almost nothing.** The actionable information is the chemical name, which is required on the label in long-form warnings and (post-2025 amendments) in new short-form warnings. Confidence: **High**.
- **Not all listed chemicals deserve the same response.** Lead, cadmium, DEHP and other phthalates, benzene, formaldehyde, hexavalent chromium, PFOA/PFOS, and asbestos are serious. Acrylamide in roasted foods and naturally occurring trace arsenic have a very different risk profile, and courts have started to push back on mandatory warnings for dietary acrylamide as unconstitutional compelled speech. Confidence: **High**.
- **Exposure route and dose matter more than the label.** A phthalate in a teething toy is a different problem than a phthalate in a shower curtain you never chew on. Children, infants, and pregnant people are higher-stakes audiences. Confidence: **High**.
- **Roughly 75% of Prop 65 settlement money since 2000 has gone to attorney fees, not safety improvements**, according to Los Angeles Times analysis cited by Consumer Reports and summarized in the Wikipedia entry. The law has become a "bounty hunter" cottage industry, which is why labels are on everything. Confidence: **High** for the ~75% figure; **Medium** that this fully explains overwarning (there are also real reformulations).
- **Look up the chemical on OEHHA's Prop 65 list and compare the exposure path to a safe-harbor level** (NSRL for cancer, MADL for reproductive harm). If the chemical is not on a list you care about, or the exposure is not on the route that matters (e.g., you don't eat your shower curtain), the label is probably regulatory noise. If it is lead in food, a phthalate in a product a child mouths, or benzene in something you inhale daily, take it seriously. Confidence: **High**.

## 1. What Prop 65 Actually Is

Proposition 65, officially the **Safe Drinking Water and Toxic Enforcement Act of 1986**, was approved by California voters in November 1986 by a 63-37 margin. It does two things: (1) it forbids businesses from knowingly discharging listed chemicals into California drinking-water sources, and (2) it requires businesses with 10+ employees to give "clear and reasonable warning" before knowingly exposing a person in California to a listed chemical ([source](https://oehha.ca.gov/proposition-65/about-proposition-65)).

The **California Office of Environmental Health Hazard Assessment (OEHHA)**, part of CalEPA, maintains the official list. As of OEHHA's plain-language page, the list has grown to **over 800 chemicals** since the first publication in 1987 and is updated at least annually; Consumer Reports and OEHHA materials both cite "more than 900" chemicals in their more recent write-ups ([source](https://oehha.ca.gov/proposition-65/general-info/proposition-65-plain-language)) ([source](https://www.consumerreports.org/toxic-chemicals-substances/why-california-thinks-your-couch-will-cause-cancer-prop-65-a5957101345/)).

A listed chemical is one that California has determined can cause cancer, birth defects, or other reproductive harm. Listings draw on evidence from the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC), the National Toxicology Program (NTP), EPA, NIOSH, and California's own qualified experts ([source](https://oehha.ca.gov/proposition-65/proposition-65-list)).

Penalties run up to **$2,500 per violation per day**, which is how the warning label became the default compliance strategy ([source](https://oehha.ca.gov/proposition-65/about-proposition-65)).

## 2. Why Almost Everything Has the Label

This is the part most consumers don't understand. The law has a unique enforcement structure that creates a strong incentive to slap a warning on anything that might conceivably contain a listed substance:

**Private enforcement (the "bounty hunter" provision).** Prop 65 lets any private party sue on behalf of the state after filing a 60-day notice with the Attorney General. If the AG does not take over, the private enforcer can proceed. The plaintiff keeps 25% of civil penalties, plus attorney fees ([source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1986_California_Proposition_65)) ([source](https://www.khlaw.com/insights/september-2025-bounty-hunter-plaintiff-claims)).

**Settlement economics.** From 1988 to 2020, private enforcers filed more than 30,000 claims targeting 100,000+ products. From 2000 to 2020, businesses paid more than **$370 million in settlements**, and **nearly 75% of that went to attorneys**, per analysis reported by Consumer Reports and summarized in the Wikipedia entry ([source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1986_California_Proposition_65)) ([source](https://www.consumerreports.org/toxic-chemicals-substances/why-california-thinks-your-couch-will-cause-cancer-prop-65-a5957101345/)).

**Asymmetric incentives.** Testing a product to prove it is below the safe-harbor threshold costs money. Defending a 60-day notice costs much more, even if you win. Slapping on a warning costs pennies. There is **no penalty for posting a warning you didn't technically need**, so companies label almost everything that could plausibly contain a listed chemical ([source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1986_California_Proposition_65)).

**Consequence: "alarm fatigue."** A Harvard Kennedy School analysis cited by Consumer Reports concluded that Prop 65 "fails miserably at distinguishing between large and small risks." When warnings are everywhere, consumers tune them out, which ironically makes the system worse at communicating real hazards. California courts have acknowledged the proliferation of "meaningless warnings" ([source](https://www.consumerreports.org/toxic-chemicals-substances/why-california-thinks-your-couch-will-cause-cancer-prop-65-a5957101345/)) ([source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1986_California_Proposition_65)).

The system has even been trimmed by the federal courts. In **May 2025**, the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of California ruled that Prop 65's warning requirement for **dietary acrylamide is unconstitutional compelled speech** under the First Amendment. In **August 2025**, a federal court permanently enjoined enforcement of the warning requirement for **titanium dioxide in cosmetics and personal care products** on the same grounds. The Attorney General has appealed the acrylamide ruling to the Ninth Circuit ([source](https://oag.ca.gov/prop65)).

## 3. How to Read a Prop 65 Label

Prop 65 warnings have been in use for decades, but the **current safe-harbor format** dates from regulations that took effect on **August 30, 2018**, and was amended again effective **January 1, 2025**. The 2018 rules introduced the now-familiar format:

1. **A warning symbol**: a black exclamation point inside a yellow equilateral triangle with a bold black outline.
2. **The signal word "WARNING"** (or, post-2025, optionally "CA WARNING" / "CALIFORNIA WARNING").
3. **The name of at least one listed chemical** that triggered the warning, for long-form warnings. Chemical names must be spelled out as on the OEHHA list, not as acronyms (e.g., "di(2-ethylhexyl)phthalate," not just "DEHP").
4. **A reference to www.P65Warnings.ca.gov** (or the food-specific subpage) so consumers can look up the chemical.

([source](https://www.p65warnings.ca.gov/business-resources/frequently-asked-questions-businesses)) ([source](https://www.acquiscompliance.com/blog/california-proposition-65-understanding-warning-label/))

A **long-form warning** reads something like: *"WARNING: This product can expose you to chemicals including [chemical name], which is known to the State of California to cause [cancer / birth defects or other reproductive harm]. For more information, go to www.P65Warnings.ca.gov."*

A **short-form warning** (used on packages with less than 5 square inches of label space) historically needed only to reference the hazard category. Starting January 1, 2025, **new short-form warnings must also name at least one chemical per endpoint** (cancer and/or reproductive harm); there is a transition window until January 1, 2028 ([source](https://www.food-safety.com/articles/10225-new-changes-to-californias-proposition-65-short-form-warning-labels)).

**The practical point:** the chemical name is the signal. A generic "This product may expose you to chemicals..." warning with no name is much less informative than one that says "lead" or "DEHP" or "acrylamide." Look for the name first.

## 4. The Chemicals That Actually Matter

Not all 900+ chemicals on the Prop 65 list are equivalent. Here are the ones OEHHA considers common enough to publish dedicated consumer fact sheets for, grouped by how seriously most toxicologists take them in everyday consumer products:

### High-concern chemicals you should take seriously

These are on the list for well-established reasons, have meaningful exposure pathways from consumer products, and warrant action:

- **Lead.** Neurotoxic at very low doses, especially in children. Found in older paint, some imported ceramics and jewelry, some vinyl and plastic products (cables, garden hoses), and trace amounts in some foods and supplements. MADL (reproductive): **0.5 μg/day**. NSRL (cancer): **15 μg/day**. These are among the lowest safe-harbor levels on the list ([source](https://oehha.ca.gov/proposition-65/general-info/current-proposition-65-no-significant-risk-levels-nsrls-maximum)).

- **Cadmium.** Heavy metal. Listed for cancer and reproductive harm. Shows up in costume jewelry, some pigments, some batteries, and cigarette smoke ([source](https://www.p65warnings.ca.gov/fact-sheets)).

- **Benzene.** Listed for cancer (leukemia) and reproductive harm. Main exposures: gasoline vapors, vehicle exhaust, tobacco smoke, some petroleum products. NSRL oral: **6.4 μg/day**; inhalation: **13 μg/day** ([source](https://www.p65warnings.ca.gov/fact-sheets/benzene)) ([source](https://oehha.ca.gov/proposition-65/general-info/current-proposition-65-no-significant-risk-levels-nsrls-maximum)).

- **Formaldehyde (gas).** Known human carcinogen (leukemia, nasopharyngeal cancer). Off-gasses from particleboard, plywood, fiberboard, some insulation, and some hair-smoothing products. The exposure that matters is **inhalation**, especially in enclosed spaces with new furniture or cabinetry. Look for CARB Phase 2, NAF (no added formaldehyde), or ULEF (ultra-low-emitting formaldehyde) labels on composite wood products ([source](https://www.p65warnings.ca.gov/fact-sheets/formaldehyde)).

- **DEHP and other phthalates.** Plasticizers used to make PVC flexible. Listed for cancer and reproductive harm; special concern for **pregnancy and infant boys**. Found in vinyl shower curtains, some upholstery, some food packaging, medical tubing, and historically in toys and childcare items (federal and California law now restrict DEHP above 0.1% in toys). Practical dodge: avoid PVC (recycle code 3) where you can ([source](https://www.p65warnings.ca.gov/fact-sheets/di2-ethylhexylphthalate-dehp)).

- **Bisphenol A (BPA).** Listed for female reproductive harm. Lines metal cans and jar lids, appears in polycarbonate plastics and thermal receipt paper. Switch to glass or stainless steel for food storage; prefer fresh over canned where practical; wash hands after handling receipts ([source](https://www.p65warnings.ca.gov/fact-sheets/bisphenol-bpa)).

- **Hexavalent chromium (Chromium VI).** Known carcinogen. Shows up in some metal platings and leather tanning residues. OEHHA proposed tighter health-protective concentration standards in 2026 ([source](https://www.certivo.com/blog-details/prop-65-hexavalent-chromium-2026-what-oehha-s-new-health-protective-concentration-means-for-manufacturers)).

- **PFOA, PFOS, and related PFAS.** "Forever chemicals." On the Prop 65 list; also a major focus of separate federal action. Exposure mainly through contaminated water, some non-stick coatings, some stain- and water-resistant textiles ([source](https://www.p65warnings.ca.gov/fact-sheets)).

- **Asbestos.** Well-established carcinogen (mesothelioma, lung cancer). Mostly legacy insulation and some imported products. A Prop 65 warning naming asbestos is a genuine alarm ([source](https://oehha.ca.gov/proposition-65/proposition-65-list)).

- **Diesel engine exhaust, tobacco smoke, cannabis smoke, wood dust.** Inhalation carcinogens. These warnings typically appear on signs in places (loading docks, parking garages, smoking areas, cabinet shops), not on products you buy. Treat them as reminders to ventilate, not as ambient panic ([source](https://www.p65warnings.ca.gov/fact-sheets)).

### Lower-concern chemicals (the "everything has a label" category)

These are on the list but are either (a) present at trace levels created by normal cooking or natural occurrence, (b) present in products where exposure is minimal (e.g., handle-only contact), or (c) actively contested in court as overreach:

- **Acrylamide.** Forms naturally when starchy plant foods are cooked at high temperature: french fries, potato chips, toasted bread crusts, roasted coffee beans, roasted nuts, breakfast cereals. EPA classifies it as "likely carcinogenic to humans" based on animal data; the federal ruling in May 2025 specifically blocked Prop 65 enforcement against dietary acrylamide warnings as a First Amendment violation (case on appeal to the Ninth Circuit). Cooking adjustments help: cook to golden, not brown; don't refrigerate raw potatoes ([source](https://www.p65warnings.ca.gov/fact-sheets/acrylamide)) ([source](https://oag.ca.gov/prop65)).

- **Naturally occurring inorganic arsenic.** Present in trace amounts in soil, water, and some foods (e.g., rice). A Prop 65 label driven by naturally occurring background levels in a food is a weaker signal than a label naming an industrial contaminant ([source](https://oehha.ca.gov/proposition-65/proposition-65-list)).

- **Titanium dioxide (inhaled form).** Permanent injunction in August 2025 against requiring Prop 65 warnings for titanium dioxide in cosmetics and personal-care products ([source](https://oag.ca.gov/prop65)).

- **Ethanol in alcoholic beverages.** Yes, "alcoholic beverages" are on the Prop 65 list. The warning on your wine bottle is not news; it is a formality ([source](https://www.p65warnings.ca.gov/fact-sheets)).

The "lower-concern" label here means lower-concern **in the context of a Prop 65 label on a consumer product**. It does not mean these substances are harmless at all doses; it means the Prop 65 warning itself is not carrying much additional information beyond what you already knew.

## 5. Exposure Route and Dose

A chemical being present is not the same as you being exposed to it. Three questions matter:

1. **Route.** Is the exposure oral (you eat or drink it), inhalation (you breathe it), or dermal (it touches your skin)? Many chemicals are much worse by one route than another. Formaldehyde is a big deal as an inhaled gas but essentially irrelevant when bound in a sealed finish. Benzene is dangerous inhaled in a fueling station; it is not leaching out of the plastic of your pump handle.
2. **Dose and frequency.** One-time use vs. daily exposure. The safe-harbor NSRL levels are calculated as daily exposure over a 70-year lifetime producing no more than one excess cancer in 100,000 people. A power tool you use twice a year is a different story than a piece of cookware you use every day ([source](https://oehha.ca.gov/proposition-65/general-info/proposition-65-plain-language)).
3. **Who.** Infants, small children, and pregnant people have higher stakes. A phthalate or BPA warning on something a toddler will put in their mouth is a real signal. The same chemical in an adult-only product used briefly is a different calculation. OEHHA's own fact sheets flag pregnancy and childhood as the special-caution windows ([source](https://www.p65warnings.ca.gov/fact-sheets/di2-ethylhexylphthalate-dehp)) ([source](https://www.p65warnings.ca.gov/fact-sheets/bisphenol-bpa)).

Consumer Reports' summary captures the intuition: *"Products touching babies' skin or mouths warrant serious consideration. Foods and daily cosmetics demand greater caution than environmental warnings at theme parks or gas stations."* ([source](https://www.consumerreports.org/toxic-chemicals-substances/why-california-thinks-your-couch-will-cause-cancer-prop-65-a5957101345/))

## 6. How to Look Up a Specific Chemical

1. **Go to the OEHHA Prop 65 list.** The full list is at [oehha.ca.gov/proposition-65/proposition-65-list](https://oehha.ca.gov/proposition-65/proposition-65-list). You can download it as PDF, Excel, or CSV. Each entry includes the chemical name, a CAS number (the global chemical ID), the date listed, and the toxicity endpoint (cancer, developmental, male reproductive, female reproductive) ([source](https://oehha.ca.gov/proposition-65/proposition-65-list)).
2. **Check for a consumer fact sheet.** OEHHA maintains about 75 plain-language fact sheets at [p65warnings.ca.gov/fact-sheets](https://www.p65warnings.ca.gov/fact-sheets). If the chemical has a fact sheet, that is the fastest way to understand why it is listed, where it shows up, and how to reduce exposure ([source](https://www.p65warnings.ca.gov/fact-sheets)).
3. **Check the safe-harbor level (NSRL or MADL).** OEHHA publishes a table at [oehha.ca.gov/proposition-65/general-info/current-proposition-65-no-significant-risk-levels-nsrls-maximum](https://oehha.ca.gov/proposition-65/general-info/current-proposition-65-no-significant-risk-levels-nsrls-maximum). Exposures below these daily dose levels are legally exempt from the warning requirement, so they are also a useful reference point for thinking about risk. Over 300 safe-harbor levels have been set ([source](https://oehha.ca.gov/proposition-65/general-info/proposition-65-plain-language)) ([source](https://oehha.ca.gov/proposition-65/general-info/current-proposition-65-no-significant-risk-levels-nsrls-maximum)).
4. **Cross-reference with the federal toxicology literature.** ATSDR (Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry) publishes Toxicological Profiles at [atsdr.cdc.gov/toxprofiles](https://www.atsdr.cdc.gov/toxprofiles/). IARC monographs (at [monographs.iarc.who.int](https://monographs.iarc.who.int/)) tell you whether a chemical is classified as Group 1 (known carcinogen), 2A (probable), 2B (possible), or 3 (not classifiable). This is useful when a Prop 65 listing is based on narrower evidence than the international consensus.
5. **Contact the manufacturer.** OEHHA specifically recommends reaching out to the manufacturer or retailer to ask *which* chemical triggered the warning and at what level, if the label is vague. Under the post-2018 rules they should already be naming it, but older labels and some short-form warnings will not ([source](https://www.p65warnings.ca.gov/)).

## 7. Decision Framework

A practical checklist for the next time you pick up a product with a Prop 65 warning:

1. **Find the chemical name on the label.** If there is no chemical named, go look it up on P65Warnings.ca.gov or contact the manufacturer. A warning with no chemical is a warning you cannot evaluate.
2. **Is the chemical on the high-concern short list?** (Lead, cadmium, DEHP and other phthalates, benzene, formaldehyde, hexavalent chromium, PFAS, asbestos, arsenic compounds, mercury, chlorinated Tris.) If yes, move to step 3 with higher alertness. If it is acrylamide in roasted food, titanium dioxide in a cosmetic, or ethanol in alcohol, the label is likely carrying less marginal information.
3. **What is the exposure route for this product?**
   - Ingestion: food, drink, cookware, anything a child mouths. Highest scrutiny.
   - Inhalation: paints, solvents, new furniture off-gassing, fuels, power-tool dust, sawing/grinding. High scrutiny if you use it often or in an enclosed space.
   - Skin contact only: cables, tool handles, hard-plastic cases, exterior of furniture. Usually low scrutiny unless the chemical is also dermally absorbed at meaningful rates or the product gets into prolonged wet contact.
4. **How often and how long will you use it?** A once-a-year item and a daily item are not the same risk.
5. **Who will use it or be near it?** Adults only, or infants/small children/pregnant people? Default to caution for the latter.
6. **Is there a practical safer alternative at a comparable price?** If yes, switching is cheap insurance. If not, the question becomes whether the real risk justifies doing without.
7. **Sanity-check the dose.** Pull the NSRL or MADL and ask whether daily use plausibly exceeds it. For most consumer products with the warning, the actual exposure is well below the safe-harbor level and the label is a liability hedge.

## 8. Red Flags vs. Green Flags

### Red flags (treat the warning as a real signal)

- **Lead named on a product a child will handle or mouth.** Especially imported ceramics, toys, vinyl products, cheap costume jewelry.
- **Phthalates (DEHP, DBP, BBP) named on a product used around infants, children, or during pregnancy** that is not explicitly compliant with CPSC/California phthalate limits.
- **Formaldehyde named on composite wood furniture or cabinetry** you will install in a small or poorly ventilated room, without CARB Phase 2 / NAF / ULEF compliance called out.
- **Benzene, toluene, xylene** named on a product you use indoors without adequate ventilation.
- **Hexavalent chromium, cadmium, asbestos** named on anything. These have few legitimate high-exposure consumer use cases.
- **Any chemical named on food, drink, or supplements** where the exposure route is direct ingestion, especially if the chemical is not a natural cooking byproduct.
- **Short-form warning with no chemical name** on a product where the category is plausibly high-risk (e.g., imported electronics, off-brand children's items, unknown-origin cookware). That is a product where the manufacturer may not have bothered to know what is in their own product.

### Green flags (label is probably regulatory noise)

- **Acrylamide named on roasted coffee, roasted nuts, or baked starchy foods.** This is a natural cooking byproduct. Federal court has already blocked enforcement of this specific warning category.
- **Titanium dioxide named on cosmetics and personal-care products.** Permanently enjoined as unconstitutional in 2025.
- **Alcoholic beverages labeled with a Prop 65 warning.** The listed "chemical" is ethanol, which is the product.
- **A warning posted at the entrance of a parking garage, gas station, hotel, amusement park, or restaurant.** These are ambient-exposure notices driven by vehicle exhaust, smoking areas, or similar; the label is not telling you anything new.
- **A warning on a product you touch briefly and only on the exterior**, where the chemical is named and the toxicology says skin contact is not the dominant route.
- **Warnings on mass-market products from major brands that already meet federal safety standards**, where the Prop 65 label is effectively a California-only legal overlay on nationally compliant goods.
